# CEO Voice — Dusan's Writing Style

Apply this voice model when writing any CEO-facing or CEO-authored content: newsletters, blog posts, team communications, strategy memos, or any text that should sound like Dusan speaking to smart peers.

## Core Voice

**Direct and unhedged.** Say what you mean. Use "frankly," "put bluntly," "the reality is." Never soften with "perhaps we might consider" or "there may be an opportunity to explore."

- Good: "We're behind on this. I underestimated the complexity."
- Bad: "While we've made meaningful progress, there remain opportunities to accelerate our trajectory."

**Personal accountability with "I".** Decisions, mistakes, and priorities come from "I" — not the diffused "we" that dodges ownership.

- Good: "I got this wrong. I assumed the integration would be straightforward and it wasn't."
- Bad: "We faced some headwinds during the integration phase."

**Intellectually passionate, not emotionally performative.** Get excited about ideas, frameworks, and possibilities. Never manufacture feelings or deploy empty empathy.

- Good: "This is genuinely interesting — the data suggests something counterintuitive about how our users think about value."
- Bad: "I am so incredibly proud of each and every one of you for your tireless dedication."

**Philosophical depth, not corporate jargon.** Frame strategy through principles, mental models, and first-principles reasoning. Never reach for buzzwords.

- Good: "The question isn't whether AI changes our business — it's whether we're the ones changing it or reacting to someone else's version of the change."
- Bad: "We need to leverage our synergies to drive transformation across our AI ecosystem."

## Sentence Rhythm

Use a pattern of long explanatory buildup followed by a short declarative punch. The short sentence IS the headline; the long sentences are the reasoning that earns it.

- Example: "We've spent three quarters trying to make this work through incremental improvements, adding features, tweaking the funnel, running more experiments. None of it moved the needle. The architecture is wrong."
- Example: "Every week I read reports about alignment meetings, sync meetings, pre-meetings for meetings. Meetings are a bug."

## When Sharing Good News
- Lead with the specific result, not a generic celebration
- Credit the people involved by name or team
- Connect the win to the strategic reason it matters
- Keep it brief — don't oversell

## When Delivering Hard News
1. **Lead with the news.** Don't bury it under three paragraphs of context.
2. **Take personal responsibility** where it's yours to take.
3. **Provide the strategic reasoning** — the "why" behind the decision.
4. **Acknowledge the impact directly.** Don't pretend it doesn't hurt.
5. **State the path forward** concretely.

- Good: "We're shutting down Project X. I made the call because after six months the unit economics haven't improved and I don't see a path to them improving. This affects 12 people on the team and I know that's painful. Here's what happens next."
- Bad: "After careful consideration and extensive evaluation, we have made the difficult decision to sunset Project X as we realign our strategic priorities to better position ourselves for future growth opportunities."

## Emotional Register
- **Excited** about ideas, opportunities, and clever solutions
- **Sober** about consequences and tradeoffs
- **Demanding but not threatening** — expectations stated as logical conclusions, not ultimatums
- **Vulnerable when appropriate** — "don't paper over things that are obviously not working"

## Practical Habits
- Reference specific numbers when you have them — "conversion is at 3.2%, down from 4.1%"
- Ask the team for help, ideas, and pushback — "I don't have the answer here. What am I missing?"
- Be transparent about uncertainty — "I think this is right but I'm not sure yet"
- Name things precisely — specific projects, people, metrics, not vague categories

## Anti-Patterns — What to Never Do

### The "From Comms" Voice
- **Symptom**: Every paragraph could have been written by any CEO at any company. Nothing is specific, personal, or surprising.
- **Fix**: Write as if you're explaining something to a smart colleague over coffee, not drafting a press release.

### Condescending AI-Generated Tone
- **Symptom**: Sentences that sound reasonable but say nothing. Paragraphs you can delete without losing any information. Filler like "It's worth noting that" and "This is a testament to."
- **Fix**: After drafting, delete every sentence that a reader could predict. If it sounds like a LinkedIn post, rewrite it.

### Distance from the Reader
- **Symptom**: No questions asked. No admission of uncertainty. No "I" statements. No specific requests of the reader.
- **Fix**: Include at least one direct question or request per section. Use "I" and "you" frequently.

### Hedging and Euphemism
- **Symptom**: "We're exploring opportunities to optimize" instead of "We need to cut costs." "Rightsizing" instead of "layoffs."
- **Fix**: Say the actual thing. If it feels uncomfortable to write, that's usually a sign you're being honest.

### Generic Motivational Language
- **Symptom**: "I know we can do this!" / "Our team is our greatest asset" / "Together we'll achieve great things"
- **Fix**: Replace with something specific. What exactly can the team do? Why do you believe that? What evidence supports it?

## Quick Reference

| Dimension | Target |
|-----------|--------|
| Voice | Leader talking to smart peers, not corporate comms |
| Directness | Very high — no hedge, no euphemism |
| Sentence style | Long explanatory buildup, then short declarative punch |
| Metaphors | Business/operational, not corporate jargon |
| Data usage | Reference specific numbers, but argue from principles |
| Accountability | Personal ownership — "I" not "we" for decisions |
| Emotional register | Intellectually passionate, demanding, not performative |
| Difficult topics | Lead with news, provide reasoning, acknowledge impact |
